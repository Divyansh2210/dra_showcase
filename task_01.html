A mid-sized Indian FMCG manufacturer currently sources 100% of its critical input, Specialty Chemical A, from an overseas supplier (Supplier X). While Supplier X offers a competitive base price, the procurement structure involves multiple cost layers including logistics, quality-related losses, and working capital implications. With increasing pressure on margins, the CFO is evaluating whether introducing a domestic supplier (Supplier Y) under a 60:40 dual-sourcing model would lead to a structurally lower total procurement cost. The decision is time-sensitive and will directly influence the upcoming financial year’s cost base. An incorrect decision is expected to result in an annual cost impact in the range of ₹8–12 crore, with a corresponding adverse effect on EBITDA margins. You are required to evaluate whether the company should continue with 100% sourcing from Supplier X or transition to a 60:40 sourcing split between Supplier X and Supplier Y. The analysis must be strictly cost-driven and based on a comprehensive Total Cost of Ownership framework. All relevant cost components must be incorporated into the evaluation, including but not limited to base purchase price, logistics and freight expenses, quality-related costs arising from defective units, working capital financing costs, and inventory carrying costs. Cost elements may be presented in different formats across data sources and should be interpreted and standardized appropriately before comparison. Quality-related costs should reflect the expected financial impact based on defect incidence and associated resolution cost. The final output must include a clear computation of effective per-unit cost for each supplier and a comparison of total annual procurement cost under both sourcing strategies. All calculations must be explicitly shown and fully auditable. You may rely only on the provided internal data files for all operational and cost inputs. For cost of capital assumptions, you are restricted to using officially published reference rates from the Reserve Bank of India. No other external sources are permitted. Assume that total demand remains constant and is fully met under both sourcing strategies. Supplier Y operates entirely in INR with no foreign exchange exposure. The analysis should be conducted on a one-year, annualized basis, and all monetary values must be reported in INR. The final output should be presented as a formal Board memo suitable for senior management review. It should open with a concise executive summary that clearly states an accept or reject recommendation on the proposed dual-sourcing strategy, supported by a direct linkage to the cost outcome. This should be followed by a structured comparison of per-unit costs, a complete annual cost evaluation under both scenarios, and a focused discussion of the key cost drivers influencing the decision. The memo should conclude with a definitive, cost-based recommendation aligned to the quantified results. The recommendation must be unambiguous and derived strictly from the computed cost comparison. Responses that rely on unstated assumptions, fail to normalize cost components, or do not adhere to the required structure will be considered invalid.

The evaluation has been carried out using a Total Cost of Ownership framework, with all cost elements standardized to ensure comparability across sourcing options. The objective is to derive a fully loaded per-unit cost for each supplier and assess the resulting annual procurement cost under both sourcing scenarios.
Logistics costs have been normalized to a per-unit basis. Supplier X’s freight, provided as a total shipment cost of ₹200,000 for 10,000 units, translates to ₹20 per unit. Supplier Y’s freight is already expressed on a per-unit basis at ₹20. This ensures consistency in cost comparison.
Quality-related costs have been incorporated using an expected value approach. Supplier X’s defect rate of 2% and replacement cost of ₹500 result in an expected cost of ₹10 per unit. Supplier Y’s defect rate of 5% and replacement cost of ₹300 result in an expected cost of ₹15 per unit. These are included directly in the per-unit cost.
The resulting pre-financing cost is ₹530 per unit for Supplier X and ₹555 per unit for Supplier Y.
Working capital implications have been incorporated using the reference rate published by the Reserve Bank of India. The effective exposure period is calculated as the sum of lead time and payment terms, resulting in 105 days for Supplier X and 25 days for Supplier Y. Applying an annualized rate of 6.5% leads to a financing cost of approximately ₹9.9 per unit for Supplier X and ₹2.5 per unit for Supplier Y.
Inventory carrying costs have been incorporated using a holding cost assumption of 10% per annum. Using lead time as a proxy for inventory exposure, this results in an additional cost of approximately ₹6.5 per unit for Supplier X and ₹1.5 per unit for Supplier Y.
After incorporating all cost components, the fully loaded per-unit cost is approximately ₹546 for Supplier X and ₹559 for Supplier Y.
Based on an annual demand of 100,000 units, the total procurement cost under a 100% Supplier X model is approximately ₹5.46 crore. Under the proposed 60:40 dual-sourcing model, total cost increases to approximately ₹5.51 crore.
Golden Answer Range: Supplier X Cost ₹540–₹551 per unit. Supplier Y Cost ₹553–₹565 per unit. Total Cost (Single Sourcing) ₹5.40–₹5.51 crore. Total Cost (Dual Sourcing) ₹5.45–₹5.56 crore. Decision: REJECT dual sourcing.
The analysis indicates that the dual-sourcing model results in a structurally higher total cost. While Supplier Y provides advantages in terms of reduced working capital and inventory exposure, these benefits are insufficient to offset its higher base price and quality-related cost impact. Accordingly, continuing with Supplier X as the sole supplier remains the more cost-efficient option under the given assumptions.
