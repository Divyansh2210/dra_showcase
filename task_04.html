ZipDash is a quick commerce operator running 12 dark stores across Bengaluru with a promised 10 minute delivery SLA. Their highest-volume dark store at HSR Layout is consistently capping throughput at 38 orders/hour against a design target of 55 orders/hour - a 31% shortfall that is directly causing SLA breaches during peak hours (7 to 9 PM). The COO must decide, before the next board review in 5 days, whether to approve a ₹18 lakh capex proposal to expand one specific stage of the dark store's fulfillment operation. A wrong stage identification wastes the entire capex budget and leaves the throughput ceiling unresolved. Using only the provided operations files, determine which single stage - picking, packing, or dispatch is the binding constraint limiting throughput at the HSR Layout dark store. Compute the steady-state throughput rate (in orders/hour) for each stage and identify the bottleneck using ZipDash's official bottleneck definition. Then evaluate whether the proposed ₹18 lakh capex, as specified in the capex proposal file, is sufficient to close the throughput gap to the 55 orders/hour design target. Use only the following four files. No external data, benchmarks, or market reports are permitted: HSR_Operations_Log_3Weeks.xlsx - Stage-level throughput logs across 3 weeks and 3 shifts DarkStore_Benchmarking_Report.pdf - Multi-city dark store benchmarking report Capex_Proposal_DispatchExpansion.xlsx - Capex model for proposed stage expansion ZipDash_OpsPolicy_Manual.pdf - Internal operations policy and definitions manual Throughput rates should be computed as averages across shifts within each valid week Only data from HSR Layout dark store is relevant to this analysis The steady-state baseline must conform to ZipDash's official definition of a valid data window as stated in the OpsPolicy Manual All cost figures are in Indian Rupees (₹ lakh) Compute the steady-state throughput rate (orders/hour) for each of the three stages - picking, packing, and dispatch Identify the single binding constraint stage, citing the relevant policy definition from the OpsPolicy Manual Confirm whether the proposed capex targets the correct bottleneck stage State whether the ₹18 lakh capex is sufficient to achieve the 55 orders/hour design target - quantify any remaining gap Provide a one-sentence Go / Conditional Go / No-Go recommendation to the COO Deliver a structured Operations Memo containing: Table 1: Stage-wise steady-state throughput (orders/hour), 3 rows × 2 columns (Stage | Throughput) Table 2: Capex sufficiency assessment (Current capacity | Post-capex capacity | Design target | Gap) Section: Bottleneck identification rationale (max 100 words, citing source document and page/row) Section: COO Recommendation (one sentence, explicitly marked Go / Conditional Go / No-Go) Round all throughput figures to one decimal place.
Lazy AI Test:

A normal model will use the 3-week aggregate average picking rate of 28.4 orders/hour from the Week Summary tab in HSR_Operations_Log_3Weeks rather than excluding the scanner-outage Week 2 flagged in the footnote, producing a picking rate 7.7 orders/hour below the correct steady-state figure of 36.1, causing it to misidentify Picking (not Dispatch) as the binding constraint and recommend ₹18 lakh capex for the wrong stage.

Expert Test:

Two experienced consultants, working independently with the same four files, would both reach the same conclusion: Dispatch is the binding constraint at 34.3 orders/hour steady-state (Weeks 1 and 3 only), the ₹18 lakh capex correctly targets Dispatch but is partially sufficient (closing capacity to 52 orders/hour, 3 orders/hour short of the 55 design target), and the recommendation is Conditional Go because the footnote exclusion and the OpsPolicy Manual Section 5.8 clean-week definition together leave no ambiguity about which data to use or which stage is the bottleneck.
1. From the Operations Log file, Sheet - Week Summary), check three weeks of data with an all-weeks combined average. Do not use the combined average. Scroll to the bottom of Sheet 2 and read the footnote. It says Week 2 (Days 8 to 14) had a scanner outage on Shift 1 and Shift 2, which artificially suppressed picking throughput. The Ops Team has flagged Week 2 as non-representative. Now open the OpsPolicy Manual and go to Section 5.8. It confirms that any week flagged by the Ops Team must be excluded from steady-state calculations. So exclude Week 2 entirely. Only use Week 1 and Week 3.

2. Calculate the correct throughput for each stage
Take the Week 1 average and Week 3 average for each stage and find their simple average.
Picking: Week 1 is 36.1, Week 3 is 36.4. Average is 36.3 orders per hour.
Packing: Week 1 is 41.0, Week 3 is 41.4. Average is 41.2 orders per hour.
Dispatch: Week 1 is 33.9, Week 3 is 34.7. Average is 34.3 orders per hour.

These are your steady-state figures. The trap figure for Picking is 28.4 (wrong - includes Week 2).

3. OpsPolicy Manual Section 5.9. It says the binding constraint is the stage with the lowest steady-state throughput. Picking is 36.3, Packing is 41.2, Dispatch is 34.3. Dispatch is the lowest. Dispatch is the bottleneck.

4. From the capex file. Sheet 1 shows the proposal targets the Dispatch stage, which is correct. It proposes adding 2 bays to the existing 4, giving 6 bays total. Post-expansion dispatch capacity is 52.0 orders per hour.

5. The design target is 55 orders per hour (from OpsPolicy Manual Page Section 5.9). Post-expansion capacity is 52. That leaves a gap of 3 orders per hour. The capex is partially sufficient - it closes most of the gap but not all of it. Per Section 5.10 of the OpsPolicy Manual, a supplementary action plan is required for the remaining gap.

6. The recommendation is Conditional Go. Approve the ₹18 lakh capex because it correctly targets the bottleneck stage and closes 86% of the throughput gap (18 out of 21 orders per hour). However, a supplementary operational plan must be submitted to close the remaining 3 orders per hour shortfall before the 55 orders per hour design target is declared achieved.
